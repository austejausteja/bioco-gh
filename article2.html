Pitfall Trapping Portfolio

by Tessa Mair, Biology BSc, Y2
15th April 2018

A) Introduction
Investigating the taxonomic relationships between different habitats is essential to a scientific understanding of both animals and the environments they live in. To make comparisons, it is necessary to collect relevant data. It is difficult to accurately monitor the biodiversity of a large habitat, especially as many land arthropods can be small, camouflaged, and difficult to find. A population sample can be estimated using pitfall traps. These simple traps are ground level and will catch any arthropods walking over them, allowing the organisms to be collected and studied further.
As stated by Brown and Matthews, “loss of biodiversity is a global issue, necessitating investigation at multiple spatial and temporal scales” (Brown and Matthews, 2016, p.3953) This highlights the importance of studies in uncovering ecological relationships. This paper also mentions pitfall trapping as a way to quantify activity of animals as well as their density (Brown and Matthews, 2016).
This study investigates the species richness between woodland and plantation habitats. This involves performing a statistical test to see whether there is a significant difference between number of individuals for each species in each habitat. These two environments differ in their physical and vegetative makeups, leading to the assumption that there will be a statistical difference in species diversity.

B) Methods
1. Site
The fieldwork was conducted in the vegetative area behind the University of Sussex library. In this site both woodland and plantation type habitats were located, necessary for the investigation.
2. Sampling
Pitfall trapping was used to collect the data. Holes were dug into the ground and plastic pint cups were placed into the ground so that the opening of the cup was level with the ground. Ethanol was put into the bottom of the cup to kill the arthropods and prevent predation. Each group put three cups at each site and marked them with a tag. The traps were collected after five days. The collected organisms were immediately taken to the lab for identification.
3. Statistics
The number of individuals per species for each sample was added up so that species diversity could be tested.
The data was tested for normal distribution using the Shapiro-Wilk test, where the sig. value can be compared to a standard level of significance of 0.05. This calculated whether the data was normally distributed.
To test whether there was a significant difference between number of individuals in species between habitats, i.e. species diversity, a Mann-Whitney U test was performed. The null hypothesis states that the species distributions are equal, a P-value comparison will reject or accept this hypothesis.

C) Results
Shapiro-Wilk was performed to test the normality of the data. For woodlands, the sig. level came out to be 0.000037, as this is less than 0.05, the data is shown to be not normally distributed for woodlands. For plantation, the sig. level came out to be 0.000036. The data is also not normally distributed for plantation (see figure 1 for data).
A Mann-Whitney U test was performed with the independent, nonparametric data sets. Results of the test shown below. The P-value came out to be 0.379, as this is greater than 0.05, there is a failure to reject the null hypothesis. There is no statistically significant difference in individuals per species between the two groups. This points to little species diversity between woodlands and plantations.

D) Discussion
1. Summary
This pitfall trap study led to a discovery that there was no significant difference between the amounts of individuals for each species between woodland and plantation habitats. This outcome suggests that the ecological differences between the two habitats are minimal. Twenty-five species of arthropods were identified during the study, and numbers of individuals in each species were similar between the two groups. The conclusion that can be drawn is that the two areas house similar amounts of individuals, and also similar diversity in species. This leads to the assumption that shelter, food sources, predation, climate, and other living factors must be analogous. Due to there being no significant difference, it is possible that there is movement/migration between the two ecosystems.
2. Validity
Data could have been improved by spreading traps over a larger area to assess a greater habitat area. Leaves and rainwater were removed from traps, some small arthropods could have been lost in the removal process, i.e. ones that were small or stuck to leaves. Errors in identification of organism species were also possible, leading to anomalies. Performing additional statistical tests would further assess the differences between the environments. E.g. species richness, where total number of individuals for each habitat is compared.
3. Implications
Pitfall trapping provides insight into arthropod ecosystems, relevant in conservational biology. Statistical testing is necessary in assessing biodiversity loss and the taxonomic welfare. However, as stated in Brown and Matthew’s study there, “exist significant difficulties in the analysis of longterm and spatially large data, especially where the methodology between researchers differs” (Brown and Matthews, 2016, p.3953). A call for a more united approach in statistical testing of the ecological sort is necessary.
Pitfall trapping can be an indicator of ecosystem nature, yet iterated in a study by Engel et al, this type of trap can be biased due to varied locomotive abilities of arthropods (Engel et al., 2017). However, another study compared pitfall trapping to quadrat testing and found that pitfalls were more successful in estimating taxonomic richness, suggesting pitfall traps are satisfactory in this field (Corti, Larned and Datry, 2013).
In a study similar to this portfolio, pitfall trapping was used and species richness was found to be extremely variable between country grain and local grain in European countries (Entling et al., 2012). However, in comparing the two, Entling et al collected data on an extremely large scale and environment types were far more variable, further stressing the irregularity in studies of this kind. With communication between researchers of this field, ecological biodiversity loss can be tackled as an issue, and relationships between habitats and their ecosystems can be successfully unveiled.
